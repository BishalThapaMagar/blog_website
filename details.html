How to realize the confusion in ultimate aim of humans?

Writter by : BISHAL THAPA

filed under : HAPPINESS

The quest to understand the ultimate aim or purpose of human life has puzzled philosophers, theologians, scientists, and thinkers for millennia. The confusion in identifying a clear, universally accepted aim arises largely because human existence is complex, multifaceted, and experienced differently by every individual and culture. Realizing this confusion involves reflecting on several key aspects: Multiplicity of Perspectives: Different disciplines and worldviews offer varying accounts of what constitutes the ultimate aim of life. Religious traditions often point to serving or connecting with a divine power or achieving spiritual salvation. For example, Islam and some theistic religions view life’s purpose as worship and devotion to God. Hindu philosophies speak of spiritual liberation (moksha) and fulfilling duties (dharma). Secular humanism, on the other hand, emphasizes human-centered values such as personal fulfillment and contributing to the common good without reliance on supernatural premises. Existentialism focuses on personal freedom and meaning-making through individual choices rather than pre-ordained purposes. This plurality naturally creates confusion as to which aim holds the “ultimate” truth. Subjectivity of Meaning: Unlike objective facts, the meaning of life cannot be proved or measured empirically. It is subjective and personal. What might seem a meaningful purpose for one person—such as family, career success, or artistic expression—may hold little significance for another. This inherent subjectivity means that the "ultimate aim" is often elusive and varies from one person to another, which adds to the overall confusion. Conflict Between Material and Spiritual Goals: Many grapple with the balance between material success (wealth, comfort, social status) and spiritual or ethical growth (love, compassion, self-realization). These goals sometimes conflict or feel disconnected, making it difficult to identify a single overriding aim. The modern emphasis on materialism often clouds deeper explorations of purpose, while spiritual approaches may seem out of touch with everyday realities. Philosophical and Existential Doubts: Philosophers like Jean-Paul Sartre and Friedrich Nietzsche brought to light the existential anxiety that arises when realizing life does not come with inherent meaning. The confusion partly stems from confronting the "absurd"—the disconnect between our search for meaning and the indifferent universe. For some, this leads to nihilism, the belief that life is meaningless, while for others, it inspires the freedom to create their own purpose. Evolution of Understanding Over Time: What people see as the ultimate aim can change with experience, age, culture, or circumstances. A young person’s goals might center on exploration and success, while an older person might prioritize wisdom or legacy. This fluidity adds complexity to identifying a fixed “ultimate aim.” Need for Personal Reflection: Recognizing the confusion itself is a step toward clarity. Engaging deeply with one’s own beliefs, values, and experiences can help individuals move beyond external conflicting views and find a more authentic sense of purpose. Philosophers like Viktor Frankl suggest that meaning is found through engagement in life’s challenges rather than abstract theorizing.
